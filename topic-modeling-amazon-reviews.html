Topic Modeling Amazon Fine Food Reviews¶

I use 20,000 Amazon food reviews from the Amazon Fine Food Reviews dataset (Kaggle) to find the main themes people write about, without any labels telling me what those themes are.

I compare three unsupervised approaches on the same data:

  1. PCA on the TF-IDF matrix, to see how much structure a small number of components can capture
  2. Truncated SVD (latent semantic analysis), which handles sparse text matrices directly
  3. NMF and LDA, two standard topic models, compared head to head

The short version of the findings: the reviews sort themselves into a few clear product categories (coffee, tea, pet food, drinks/snacks), NMF gives the cleanest topics on this data, and text cleaning choices matter a lot more than the choice of model.

Setup¶

Everything here is pandas, scikit-learn, and matplotlib. The dataset file Reviews.csv comes from Kaggle and isn't committed to the repo because of its size.

Data and text cleaning¶

I keep the first 20,000 reviews to keep runtimes reasonable and drop any rows with missing text. The cleaning function lowercases everything, strips out non-letter characters, and drops tokens of two characters or fewer. That last step also happens to remove br fragments left over from <br> HTML tags in the raw reviews, which would otherwise show up as a fake "topic" later on.

def preprocess_text(text):
    text = text.lower()
    text = re.sub(r'[^a-z\s]', ' ', text)
    text = re.sub(r'\s+', ' ', text).strip()
    tokens = [t for t in text.split() if len(t) > 2]
    return ' '.join(tokens)

df['clean_text'] = df['Text'].apply(preprocess_text)

print("Preprocessing complete.")

TF-IDF representation¶

TF-IDF weights each word by how often it appears in a review, discounted by how common it is across all reviews. Words like "coffee" get high weights in coffee reviews, while filler words that show up everywhere get pushed down. I cap the vocabulary at 5,000 terms for the PCA section (PCA needs a dense matrix, so memory is the constraint) and use sublinear_tf so a word appearing 20 times doesn't count 20x as much as one appearance.

Part 2: Latent semantic analysis with Truncated SVD¶

Truncated SVD does the same kind of dimensionality reduction as PCA but works on the sparse matrix directly, so I can afford a bigger vocabulary (10,000 terms). Applied to TF-IDF this is latent semantic analysis. I normalize the output vectors, which is the standard LSA pipeline in scikit-learn.

SVD recovers the same broad themes: a generic sentiment topic (like, good, great), coffee, pet food, and tea. The downside is bleed-through. Because SVD components can mix signs and aren't constrained to be non-negative, words like "coffee" leak into the pet food topic. That's the main motivation for trying NMF next.

Part 3: NMF topic modeling¶

Non-negative matrix factorization adds one constraint: every weight has to be zero or positive. No cancellation between words means each topic is a straight additive bundle of terms, which usually reads much cleaner than SVD components. I run it on the same 10,000-term TF-IDF matrix with 5 topics.

In [26]:

n_top_words = 10

nmf = NMF(n_components=num_topics, random_state=42)
X_nmf = nmf.fit_transform(svd_tfidf_matrix)

print("=== NMF Topics ===")
for i, topic in enumerate(nmf.components_):
    top_idx = topic.argsort()[:-n_top_words - 1:-1]
    top_words = [svd_vocab[idx] for idx in top_idx]
    print(f"NMF Topic {i+1}: {', '.join(top_words)}")

Part 4: LDA topic modeling¶

LDA is a probabilistic model: it treats each review as a mix of topics and each topic as a distribution over words. It's built on word counts rather than TF-IDF weights, so I switch to a CountVectorizer with the same vocabulary settings for a fair comparison.

Raw counts give frequent words more influence than TF-IDF does, and with only 20,000 short documents LDA doesn't have a lot to work with. On this dataset NMF wins on interpretability.

Part 6: What do customers care about, and how do they feel about it?¶

Finding topics is only half the job. The dataset also has a 1-5 star rating for every review, so I can connect the two: assign each review to its strongest NMF topic, then look at how big each topic is and how customers rate it. That turns "here are five word clusters" into "here's what customers talk about most and where the complaints are."

df['topic'] = X_nmf.argmax(axis=1) + 1

    top3 = [svd_vocab[j] for j in topic.argsort()[:-4:-1]]

summary = df.groupby('topic').agg(

In this sample, coffee is the largest product category at about 16% of reviews, drinks like juice and soda draw the most complaints (3.81 average rating, with 20% of their reviews at 1-2 stars), and tea is rated most positively at 4.35. Over half of all reviews land in a generic praise topic (great, product, good), which is a known quirk of review data: most people who bother to write are happy, and their language isn't product-specific. This is the kind of output a product or marketing team could actually act on, like digging into why drink reviews skew negative.

Takeaways¶

  • 20,000 unlabeled reviews reduce to a handful of clear product categories: coffee, tea, pet food, and drinks/snacks
  • NMF produced the most interpretable topics; SVD had themes bleeding into each other and LDA let high-frequency filler words dominate
  • Linking topics to star ratings shows which categories customers discuss most and where negative reviews concentrate, which is what makes this useful beyond the model itself
  • Preprocessing mattered as much as the model. Dropping short tokens removed leftover <br> HTML fragments that otherwise formed a junk topic
  • Explained variance is a misleading quality metric for text. Components with under 1% variance were still perfectly readable
