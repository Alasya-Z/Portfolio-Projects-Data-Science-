Data cleaning and Preparation:

Our dataset comes from the 2021 American Community Survey (ACS), accessed through IPUMS USA as a custom microdata extract. IPUMS is an online platform that provides access to public-use census and survey data for research purposes. To get the data, we used the IPUMS extract builder, where we selected variables related to health insurance coverage, household income, employment, citizenship, and other demographic factors. After choosing the variables we needed — like HHINCOME, HCOVANY, EMPSTAT, RACE, and more — IPUMS generated a customized CSV file for download. This file included thousands of individual records and was ready for import into R for cleaning and analysis.

To prepare the data for analysis, we began by filtering out invalid responses and missing values. For example, we removed cases with HHINCOME coded as 9999999 and dropped rows where variables like sex, marital status, employment status, veteran status, or Hispanic origin had placeholder codes like 9 (which typically represents “not in universe” or “missing”). After cleaning, we recoded several categorical variables into simpler binary formats — such as turning employment status into 1 for employed and 0 for not, and the same logic for veteran status as well.

# Cleaning and recode
data <- hcov_data %>%
  filter(
    HHINCOME != 9999999,
    SEX != 9,
    MARST != 9,
    HISPAN != 9,
    !EMPSTAT %in% c(0, 9),
    !VETSTAT %in% c(0, 9)
  ) %>%
  mutate(
    has_insurance = ifelse(HCOVANY == 2, 1, 0),
    private_insurance = HCOVPRIV,
    public_insurance  = HCOVPUB,
    marital_status    = MARST,
    SEX = case_when(SEX == 1 ~ "Male", SEX == 2 ~ "Female"),
    marital_status = case_when(MARST %in% 2:8 ~ 0, TRUE ~ 1),   # 1 = married, 0 = not
    HISPAN = case_when(HISPAN %in% 2:4 ~ 1, TRUE ~ 0),          # 1 = Hispanic
    EMPSTAT = case_when(EMPSTAT %in% 2:3 ~ 0, TRUE ~ 1),        # 1 = employed
    VETSTAT = case_when(VETSTAT == 2 ~ 1, VETSTAT == 1 ~ 0, TRUE ~ NA_real_) # 1 = veteran
  ) %>%
  select(-HCOVANY, -HCOVPRIV, -HCOVPUB, -MARST)

Exploratory Data Analysis

Before jumping into formal models, we’ll explore some basic patterns in the data to get a clearer picture of who has health insurance and who doesn’t. We’ve already cleaned the data by removing invalid responses, simplifying variables, and renaming unclear columns for clarity.

We’ll focus on the new variable has_insurance, which tells us whether someone has any form of health coverage — either public or private. This variable is coded as:

  • 1 = has insurance

  • 0 = no insurance

We’ll also explore how coverage varies across key demographic and socioeconomic factors, including:

  • HHINCOME: total household income

  • EMPSTAT: employment status (employed or not)

  • RACE and HISPAN: race and Hispanic/Latino origin

  • VETSTAT: veteran status

  • AGE, SEX, and marital_status: core demographic characteristics

To visualize these relationships, we’ll use bar plots and calculate proportions to see which groups have the highest rates of uninsurance. This will help us spot any potential disparities and guide which variables may be important in the modeling stage.
Statistical Hypothesis:

To test the relationship between health insurance coverage and demographic/socioeconomic factors, we made the following hypotheses:

Null hypothesis (H₀): None of the predictors are significantly associated with whether someone has insurance.

(H₀) :B1 =B2 ​⋯ Bk = 0 (no prediction matters)

Alternative hypothesis (H₁): At least one predictor is significantly associated with insurance status.

(H₁) : at least one Bj ≠ 0 (no prediction matters)

We will test these hypotheses using logistic regression, which is appropriate given that our outcome variable (has_insurance) is binary (1 = has insurance, 0 = no insurance).

Further investigation:

In this chunk of the data, we’re looking at the bottom 30 income levels where the proportion_uninsured is zero meaning everyone in these groups reported having health insurance, even if they had very little or negative income. For example, households earning as little as –$16,800 or barely over $1,000 all had full coverage.

This seems counterintuitive at first — why would people with barely any income all have insurance? One likely explanation is government programs like Medicaid. These individuals may qualify for public health coverage due to their income being well below the poverty line. So in this case, being extremely low-income may actually increase their chances of being insured, since they likely qualify for safety-net programs.

That said, we haven’t yet seen the tipping point where people stop qualifying for public programs but still can’t afford private insurance. To fully understand that drop-off, we’ll need to keep scanning higher income ranges where the proportion uninsured might start rising again.

Explaining logistic regression:

All of the predictors in the model are statistically significant, but some have a much stronger practical impact than others. Being a veteran shows the highest positive effect, with a coefficient close to 1, meaning veterans are nearly guaranteed to have health insurance. This makes sense given the benefits available through VA healthcare and other federal programs for those who have served. Employment and marital status also increase the likelihood of being insured, which reflects how many people rely on coverage through work or a spouse. On the other hand, being Hispanic is associated with a lower chance of having insurance, even when accounting for factors like income and job status.

This supports what we found earlier in our exploratory analysis. Men are also less likely than women to be insured. Age has a positive effect, which is expected since older individuals are more likely to qualify for Medicare, and in some cases, prioritize getting insured. While income does have a statistically significant effect, it barely shifts the outcome in practical terms. That’s likely because people with very low incomes may qualify for public options like Medicaid, which helps reduce gaps in coverage. Of course, that doesn’t mean access is equal there’s still more work to be done to expand and strengthen safety net programs, but statistically, income alone doesn’t appear to carry as much weight in this model as other factors like veteran status or employment.
Final conclusion:

Based on the results of our logistic regression, we reject the null hypothesis and find strong statistical evidence that several demographic and socioeconomic factors are significantly associated with whether someone has health insurance. Veteran status, employment, and marital status show the strongest positive effects, meaning people in those groups are much more likely to be insured. On the other hand, being Hispanic, male, or unemployed is linked to a lower likelihood of having coverage, even after controlling for income and age.

Race also plays a meaningful role Black and White individuals differ in their predicted probabilities of being insured. While income does matter, its effect is small compared to other factors, likely because programs like Medicaid help cover many low-income individuals. Altogether, the analysis highlights how access to health insurance is shaped by a mix of structural and demographic factors. These patterns point to specific communities such as Hispanic Americans, some racial minorities, and men that could benefit from more targeted outreach or expanded public options.
